Section 3.8: Lie Group Connection

The Killing form introduced in Section 3.6 acts as an inner product on the Lie algebra, that is, on the tangent space to the Lie group at the identity. As discussed in Section 3.2, vectors in the Lie algebra extend to left-invariant vector fields on the Lie group. Thus, the Killing form extends to a metric on the Lie group, which is constant on left-invariant vector fields by construction. Given a metric, we can construct its Leviâ€“Civita connection and curvature.

The Leviâ€“Civita connection is uniquely determined by the requirements that it be (a connection that is) metric compatible and torsion free. The connection \(\nabla\) is metric compatibile if

\[ Z\left(B(X,Y)\right) = \nabla_Z\left(B(X,Y)\right) = B\left(\nabla_Z X, Y\right) + B\left(X, \nabla_Z Y\right) \tag{3.8.1} \]

that is, if the derivative of â€œdotâ€ is zero (\(\nabla_Z B = 0\)). However, we know from (3.6.14) that the Killing form is constant when acting on left-invariant vector fields, so that both sides of (3.8.1) are in fact zero. The connection is torsion free if

\[ [\nabla_X, \nabla_Y]f = \nabla_{[X,Y]}f = [X,Y](f) \tag{3.8.2} \]

for any function \(f\). In both expressions, we have used the fact that

\[ \nabla_X f = X(f) \tag{3.8.3} \]

for any connection, that is, the covariant derivative of a function is just its directional derivative (which is \(\vec{X} \cdot \vec{\nabla} f\) in the language of vector calculus).

Putting these properties together, we can compute 1

\[ \begin{align*} B(\nabla_X Y, Z) &= B([X,Y], Z) + B(\nabla_Y X, Z) \\ &= B([X,Y], Z) - B(X, \nabla_Y Z) \\ &= B([X,Y], Z) - B(X, [Y,Z]) - B(X, \nabla_Z Y) \\ &= B([X,Y], Z) - B(X, [Y,Z]) + B(\nabla_Z X, Y) \\ &= B([X,Y], Z) - B(X, [Y,Z]) + B([Z,X], Y) + B(\nabla_X Z, Y) \\ &= B([X,Y], Z) - B(X, [Y,Z]) + B([Z,X], Y) - B(Z, \nabla_X Y) \tag{3.8.4} \end{align*} \]

which we can rearrange to

\[ 2 B(\nabla_X Y, Z) = B([X,Y], Z) - B([Y,Z], X) + B([Z,X], Y) = B([X,Y], Z) \tag{3.8.5} \]

where we have used the cyclic property (3.6.6) of the Killing form to cancel the last two expressions. Finally, since \(Z\) is arbitrary (and \(B\) is nondegenerate), we can conclude that

\[ \nabla_X Y = \frac{1}{2} [X,Y]. \tag{3.8.6} \]

Section 3.9: Lie Group Curvature

Given the formula (3.8.6) for the natural connection in a Lie group, it is straightforward to compute the curvature. We have

\[ \begin{align*} R(X, Y)Z &= \nabla_X \nabla_Y Z - \nabla_Y \nabla_X Z - \nabla_{[X,Y]} Z \\ &= \frac{1}{4} [X, [Y, Z]] - \frac{1}{4} [Y, [X, Z]] - \frac{1}{2} [[X, Y], Z] \\ &= \frac{1}{4} [[X, Y], Z] - \frac{1}{2} [[X, Y], Z] = -\frac{1}{4} [[X, Y], Z] \tag{3.9.1} \end{align*} \]

where we have used the Jacobi identity in the penultimate equality.

As an example, consider the Lie group \(SO(3)\). Introducing the component notation

\[ R(u, v)w = R^a{}_{bcd} w^b u^c v^d e_a \tag{3.9.2} \]

where \(\{e_a\} = \{r_x, r_y, r_z\}\) is a basis of \(\mathfrak{so}(3)\) and repeated dummy indices are summed over (the Einstein summation convention), we can compute \(R(r_x, r_y)r_z = 0\), so that \(R^a{}_{zxy} = 0\), and

\[ R(r_x, r_y)r_x = -\frac{1}{4} r_y \tag{3.9.3} \]

so that \(R^y{}_{xxy} = -\frac{1}{4}\). Since our basis is orthonormal (and Euclidean), we can freely raise and lower indices, so

\[ R^{xy}{}_{xy} = \frac{1}{4} \tag{3.9.4} \]

(using the antisymmetry of the Riemann tensor components, with no sum over \(x\) or \(y\)), leading to

\[ R^x{}_x = \frac{1}{2} = R^y{}_y = R^z{}_z \tag{3.9.5} \]

for the Ricci tensor (\(R^a{}_b = R^{ma}{}_{mb}\)) and finally \(R = \frac{3}{2}\) for the Ricci scalar (\(R = R^m{}_m\)). 1 Not surprisingly, the curvature is constant; \(SO(3)\) has (locally) the geometric structure of \(\mathbb{RP}^3\) (which is that of \(\mathbb{S}^3\)), not merely its topology.

As this argument suggests, Lie groups are manifolds with constant curvature; they are symmetric spaces. 2

1 The numerical value of \(R\) depends on the conventional scale chosen for the Killing form and carries no geometric information.

2 More precisely, these descriptions are equivalent in the compact, connected case.
